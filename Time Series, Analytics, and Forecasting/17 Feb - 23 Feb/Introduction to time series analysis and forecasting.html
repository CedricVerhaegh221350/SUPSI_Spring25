Something is easy to forecast if:

we have a good understanding of the factors that contribute to it
there is a lot of data available
the future is somewhat similar to the past
ID assumption: samples are identically distributed
the forecasts must not affect the quantity we are trying to forecast.
self-fulfilling prophecies (election polls)
controlled systems

Dealing with uncertainty

Aleatoric (alea=latin for dice) or data uncertainty. This is the irreducible uncertainty that we cannot reduce by increasing observations.
Epistemic (epistḗmē=science) or model uncertainty. This uncertainty can be reduced by increasing observed data, and as such, can be reduced
Example: we can always decompose a signal in two parts: a modeled signal and an error

Embeddings

With the word embedding we refer to the practice of rearranging a time series xxx﻿ into a matrix, or a tensor, by stacking windows of the signal. This is a very common practice in time series analysis since usually values of the signals close in time are “similar”.

Single vs multi-step forecasts

Most of the forecasting methods exploit information contained in the most recent historical values to predict the future. We usually use the notation xt∣t−kx_{t\vert t-k}xt∣t−k​﻿ to indicate the kkk﻿ most recent values
xt∣t−k=[xt−kT,xt−k+1T,...xtT]Tx_{t\vert t-k} = [x_{t-k}^T, x_{t-k+1}^T,...x_t^T]^T xt∣t−k​=[xt−kT​,xt−k+1T​,...xtT​]T
A forecasting function predicting the next step of the sequence can then be written:
x^t+1=f(xt∣t−k)\hat{x}_{t+1} = f(x_{t\vert t-k})x^t+1​=f(xt∣t−k​)
where x^\hat{x} x^﻿ stands for a forecasted value of the time series xxx﻿.
We’re usually interested in predicting multiple steps ahead of the time series. Two main strategies are transversal to most of forecasting methodologies:

Recursive prediction

In recursive prediction the forecasting model is trained to predict just one step ahead x^t+1\hat{x}_{t+1}x^t+1​﻿, and recursively substitute the prediction of the next step in its feature set.
This is the strategy adopted by
exponential smoothing
ARIMAX
state space models
Machine learning methods can also use this strategy
fit just one model
simple implementation
prediction errors tend to accumulate, stability is usually enforced

Direct multistep prediction

We fit multiple models, each one for a different step ahead
x^t+1=f1(xt∣t−k)x^t+2=f2(xt∣t−k)...x^t+T=fT(xt∣t−k)\begin{aligned} \hat{x}_{t+1} &= f_1(x_{t\vert t-k})\\ \hat{x}_{t+2} &= f_2(x_{t\vert t-k})\\ &...\\ \hat{x}_{t+T} &= f_T(x_{t\vert t-k}) \end{aligned}x^t+1​x^t+2​x^t+T​​=f1​(xt∣t−k​)=f2​(xt∣t−k​)...=fT​(xt∣t−k​)​
simple implementation
temporal consistency could be lost since models are independent

Endogenous and exogenous features

endogenous: features crafted from the same time series we’re trying to predict, e.g. lags or moving averages of the signal
exogenous: other signals, metadata, categorical features that might be relevant for the prediction
